The Arena: Playing by the rules — original

Students will use this scene to connect everyday media experiences with the course's big questions about information systems, power, and how we are formed by what we consume and create. This fits especially well in The Arena, where students practice perspective-taking and encounter difference.

Learning aims

By the end of this discussion, Seekers will be able to:

1.Identify how the social-rating system in "Nosedive" shapes characters' beliefs, behaviors, and sense of self, connecting to the idea that "information doesn't just inform, it forms" (Course Outcome 2).
2.Analyze how power, access, and social consequences operate in the "Nosedive" world, and draw parallels to real-world platforms and reputational systems (Course Outcome 8).
3.Reflect critically on their own participation in contemporary information and reputational systems, demonstrating metacognitive awareness of personal habits and biases (Course Outcome 9).
4.Engage respectfully with classmates' differing interpretations of the episode, practicing transepistemic curiosity (Course Outcome 10).

Instructions

Context

In this Arena Exploration, you will enter the world of Black Mirror: "Nosedive" to examine how systems of information, ratings, and reputation shape what people see as possible—and who they are allowed to become.

Step 1 – Watch

  1. Watch the Nosedive trailer
  2. Watch the assigned clip in which the protagonist is helped by the truck driver

Step 2 – Individual reflection post (~200 words)

System of thinking: What "rules of the game" seem to govern everyday life in the Nosedive world? How do ratings, devices, and interfaces function as an information system that tells people what—and who—has value?

Formation and consequences: Choose one character. How has this system formed their desires, fears, or sense of self? Where do you see harm or flourishing, and for whom?

Your own world: Identify one system you participate in (social media, course evaluations, Uber/Lyft ratings, follower counts, academic metrics, or AI recommender systems). In what ways do you willingly inhabit it, and in what ways do you feel pressured? How might it be forming you?

Peer exchange (optional)

Name one place where you see the system differently than a classmate does, and ask a genuine question that invites them to think further. Aim for respectful engagement, intellectual humility, and curiosity.

Accessibility: the necessary detour

What happened — and why it matters for your workshop

During the AI-assisted redesign conversation, an accessibility issue surfaced that had not been identified in the original activity design: the assigned trailer for Black Mirror: "Nosedive" lacks closed captions on YouTube, and no alternative transcript was available.

Rather than bypassing this issue, the conversation paused the redesign to work through it — modeling exactly the kind of accessibility-first thinking that WCAG 2.1 and Universal Design for Learning require.

WCAG 2.1 criteria implicated

1.2.2 (Level A)Captions required for all prerecorded synchronized media. The trailer lacked these entirely.
1.2.3 (Level A)Audio description or full media alternative required. A written scene description satisfies this criterion and can be created by the instructor.
1.2.5 (Level AA)Audio description specifically required for prerecorded video. YouDescribe (free, youDescribe.org) allows adding description tracks to YouTube videos without downloading.

Tools identified

Canvas StudioAuto-captioning with manual review. Best integrated option for Canvas LMS users. Note: downloading YouTube content for re-upload requires copyright review.
YouDescribeFree audio description tool for YouTube videos. No download required. From the Smith-Kettlewell Eye Research Institute.
Rev.comHuman-generated captions, per-minute pricing. Practical for one-off use cases.
3Play MediaUniversity-grade captioning with Canvas integration. High accuracy with human review.
WAVE / CanvasFor auditing the activity instructions themselves (not video content).

The workshop takeaway

Accessibility is not a finishing step. It surfaces real constraints that require real decisions. The question isn't "can I use this resource?" but "what do I owe my students, and what's the feasible path to meeting that obligation?"

This situation also illustrates a UDL principle: providing a written alternative to video content (Multiple Means of Representation) is not a diminished option — it may serve some learners better than the video itself, and it satisfies WCAG 1.2.3 immediately.

The redesigned activity includes a written scene description as an alternative entry point, a Canvas Studio captioning note, and a reference to YouDescribe for audio description — all addressed directly in the Accessibility Note.

The Arena: Playing by the rules — redesigned

Description

Charlie Brooker chose "Black Mirror" as his series title because that's what any screen looks like when it goes dark: a surface that shows you yourself. We use our screens as mirrors constantly — to check our appearance, our social standing, our sense of how we're doing. Most of the time the mirror feels helpful, even neutral. Nosedive asks what happens when the mirror doesn't just reflect — but judges, ranks, and ultimately forms who we are allowed to become.

In this Arena Exploration, you will enter the world of Black Mirror: "Nosedive" to examine how systems of information, ratings, and reputation shape what people see as possible — and who they are allowed to become. In Step 3, you will have the option to use an AI tool as a different kind of mirror: one that reflects your thinking back to you, asks questions your reflection didn't ask, and surfaces what you may have left in the dark.

Accessibility note: The trailer has been captioned using Canvas Studio and is available with closed captions. A YouDescribe audio description track and a written scene description are also available as alternative entry points. If you encounter any barriers accessing the video content, please contact the instructor. All prompts and instructions are screen-reader compatible.

Learning aims

By the end of this activity, Seekers will be able to:

1.Identify how the social-rating system in "Nosedive" shapes characters' beliefs, behaviors, and sense of self, connecting to the idea that "information doesn't just inform, it forms" (Course Outcome 2).
2.Analyze how power, access, and social consequences operate in the "Nosedive" world, drawing parallels to real-world platforms and reputational systems (Course Outcome 8).
3.Reflect critically on their own participation in contemporary information and reputational systems, demonstrating metacognitive awareness of personal habits and biases (Course Outcome 9).
4.Engage respectfully with classmates' differing interpretations of the episode and its implications, practicing transepistemic curiosity (Course Outcome 10).
5.newEngage an AI tool as a collaborative partner in metacognitive reflection, using iterative dialogue to surface unexamined assumptions in their own thinking (AI Literacy Competencies 2.3, 3.1, 3.3).
6.newMaintain human agency and critical judgment in AI dialogue, recognizing AI outputs as starting points for deeper inquiry rather than conclusions (AI Literacy Competencies 1.2, 2.4).
7.newRecognize AI systems as socio-technical formations that, like the Nosedive rating system, shape what users see, value, and become (AI Literacy Frame 3).

Time on task

60–90 minutes (including video, reflection, and Step 3 option)

Instructions

Every screen is a black mirror. When it's on, it shows you the world; when it's off, it shows you yourself. In this activity, you've been analyzing how a fictional system of screens forms people — their desires, their fears, their sense of what's possible. In Step 3, we're inviting you to use a screen — an AI — as a different kind of mirror: one that reflects your thinking back to you, asks questions your reflection didn't ask, and surfaces what you may have left in the dark.

Step 1 – Watch

  1. Watch the Nosedive trailer (captioned; written scene description available in Canvas)
  2. Watch the assigned clip in which the protagonist is helped by the truck driver

Step 2 – Individual reflection post (~200 words)

System of thinking: What "rules of the game" seem to govern everyday life in the Nosedive world? How do ratings, devices, and interfaces function as an information system that tells people what — and who — has value?

Formation and consequences: Choose one character. How has this system formed their desires, fears, or sense of self? Where do you see harm or flourishing, and for whom?

Your own world: Identify one system you participate in (social media, course evaluations, Uber/Lyft ratings, follower counts, academic metrics, or AI recommender systems). In what ways do you willingly inhabit it, and in what ways do you feel pushed or pressured? How might this system be forming you — your attention, your relationships, your sense of what "counts"?

You are encouraged to reference specific details from the clip — dialogue, gestures, camera shots, colors — to support your claims.

Step 3 – Deepen your reflection

After completing your post, choose one of the following options:

Option A — Written reflection only

Review your post and write a brief follow-up paragraph (75–100 words) naming one question your post left unanswered. What aspect of the system, or of your own participation in it, did you find hardest to articulate? Submit this alongside your original post.

Option B — AI as reflective surface recommended

You may complete this reflection without an AI partner, and that is a valid and valuable choice. However, we want to name something: using an AI as a reflective surface — not to improve your writing, but to surface what you may not yet be seeing in your own thinking — is itself an act of epistemic inquiry. We encourage you to try it.

After writing your original post, open a conversation with an AI tool — Claude (claude.ai), Perplexity (perplexity.ai), or ChatGPT (chatgpt.com) — and share your reflection. Then use one or more of the following prompts:

  • "Based on what I've written, what assumptions about this system am I making that I haven't questioned?"
  • "What might I be avoiding or not fully seeing about how this system is forming me?"
  • "Where does my reflection feel most certain? Push back on that certainty."
  • "If the system I described could speak for itself, what would it say in its own defense?"

A note on agency: AI tools tend toward affirming elaboration. If the AI only agrees with or expands on what you've already said, push back. Try: "What am I not seeing? What's the strongest objection to my analysis?" Maintaining that pressure is itself part of the exercise — and an important AI literacy skill.

You are not asking the AI to improve or rewrite your reflection. If you find yourself asking the AI to polish or restructure your writing, you've stepped out of the mirror and into the salon.

After your dialogue, write a brief follow-up paragraph (75–100 words) that names one thing the AI surfaced that you hadn't considered, and whether, on reflection, you agree with it.

Peer exchange (optional for all)

You are not required to reply to a classmate's post, but if you do: name one place where you see the system differently, and ask a genuine question that invites them to think further. Aim for respectful engagement, intellectual humility, and curiosity about how others experience similar systems in their lives.

Submission

  • Your original reflection post (~200 words)
  • Your Step 3 follow-up paragraph (~75–100 words)
  • If Option B: a brief note (1–2 sentences) indicating which AI tool you used
